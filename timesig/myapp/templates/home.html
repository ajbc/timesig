The Shape of Time: Identifying Time Signatures in Narrative Form

An Overview of "Time Signatures"

In a novel, time does not often move evenly or linearly–––a single paragraph in García Márquez's One Hundred Years of Solitude jumps several decades while in Proust's In Search of Lost Time, 15 pages are devoted to a single moment of eating a madeleine. In this project, we are interested the kind of language is used to talk about time and what is the shape/speed/pace of this language in a given text. Tracking what we call the "time signature" of a text, we use explicit references to time passing in order to divide up a text, and then use probabilistic methods to analyze the relationships to the kinds of words that cluster around changes in novel time and how these temporal patterns are distributed in a text. We hope to explore how "time signature" offers one lens into the shape of time in a novel and how such "time signatures" might be used to predict other aspects of a text such as author, genre, format, and place of publication.

How You Can Participate

Right now we have identified possible time anchors, but we need your help to identify the difference in time between anchors––for example, tagging a reference to "two-o'clock" as two hours ahead in time from a previous reference. To help contribute to the project, please register as a user, and then follow instructions listed on the project details page. Contributors to the project will be credited for their participation.
